# Botim Pots — Product Overview

## 1. What are we trying to achieve?

Botim Pots gives users a way to mentally and visually ring-fence money inside their Botim wallet for specific goals — without needing a separate savings account or moving money out of the app.

A user creates a "pot", gives it a name and an emoji (e.g. 🕌 Hajj Fund, 💻 MacBook, 🎓 Tuition), and sets a target amount. They then move money into it incrementally. The pot tracks progress toward the goal with a visual progress bar, and the wallet surface always shows how much is genuinely spendable versus how much is earmarked.

The key mechanic: the wallet **total never changes** when you move money into a pot from your existing balance — only the **spendable** portion shrinks. This makes it psychologically clear that the money is "yours but not for spending."

---

## 2. Why?

Most digital wallets show a single balance number. Users who want to save toward multiple goals at once have no native tool to do this — they either mentally track it, use notes apps, or keep multiple accounts. None of these work well.

The behavioural insight behind Pots is **mental accounting** — people naturally think about money in buckets ("rent money", "holiday money"), but their apps don't reflect that. By making those buckets explicit and visible in the wallet, users are less likely to accidentally spend money they've mentally reserved.

This also deepens engagement with the Botim Money wallet. A user who has pots set up has a reason to come back regularly — to top up, check progress, and feel the reward of moving toward a goal. It increases stickiness without requiring a fundamentally new financial product.

---

## 3. For who?

The primary user is a **Botim Money wallet holder** who:

- Has regular income flowing through the app and wants to be more intentional about where it goes
- Is saving toward one or more near-to-medium-term goals (a device, a trip, a religious obligation like Hajj, a family milestone)
- Isn't the type to set up a formal savings plan or fixed deposit — they want flexibility and low friction
- May be a remittance-first user who also wants to start building financial habits within the same app they already trust

Secondarily, it serves users who are **new to budgeting** and benefit from a simple, visual structure — seeing a progress bar move is more motivating than watching a balance number change.
